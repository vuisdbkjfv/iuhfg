About World Refugee Day

World Refugee Day is observed annually on June 20 to honor refugees, recognize their strength, and raise awareness about the protection and support they deserve. It’s a day to learn, reflect, and take practical steps to support people forced to flee.

Why it matters

Refugees are people escaping conflict, persecution, or disaster. Many rebuild their lives, contribute to communities, and keep culture and memory alive, even after immense loss.

Historical lens

The modern refugee system was shaped by the mass displacement of WWI and WWII, which spurred early international cooperation and later global refugee protections.

World War I displacement

Context

Front lines and civilian flight

WWI’s shifting fronts across Europe, the Middle East, and Africa led civilians to flee combat, occupation, and shortages. Border changes and empire collapses destabilized entire regions.

Impact

Internally displaced and cross-border movement

Millions moved within and across borders, often without formal support systems. Relief efforts were fragmented, laying groundwork for later humanitarian coordination.

Human stories

Families navigated scarce food, epidemics, and harsh winters on foot and by rail. Letters, diaries, and camp records show the resilience of people preserving language, craft, and faith.

Aftermath

Post-war treaties and redrawn borders created new minorities and stateless persons, challenging governments to address rights and citizenship in the interwar years.

World War II displacement

Scale

Global displacement on an unprecedented level

WWII uprooted tens of millions across Europe, Asia, and North Africa. Forced labor, ethnic persecution, and mass violence created complex refugee flows during and after the war.

Response

International relief and resettlement

Early multinational relief efforts and post-war resettlement programs emerged, informing the creation of modern refugee protections and international institutions in the following decade.

Survival

Refugees survived through mutual aid, clandestine routes, and support from local communities, faith groups, and relief networks—often under extreme risk.

Legacy

Post-war conventions and refugee status definitions grew from lessons learned, shaping rights to non-refoulement, asylum, education, and work.

FAQs

What is the difference between a refugee and an asylum seeker?

A refugee is someone recognized as needing protection across borders due to persecution or conflict. An asylum seeker is someone who has applied for refugee status and awaits a decision.

How do WWI and WWII relate to modern refugee protections?

The scale and complexity of displacement in both wars catalyzed international cooperation, laying foundations for post-war conventions, dedicated agencies, and global norms.

What challenges do refugees face today?

Access to legal protection, safe housing, healthcare, education, and dignified work—alongside risks of exploitation, xenophobia, and prolonged uncertainty during status processing.

How can individuals make a meaningful difference?

Support local resettlement groups, donate to trusted organizations, advocate for fair policies, volunteer skills, and amplify refugee voices in your community.

Key facts

Terminology

Refugee vs. internally displaced

Refugees cross international borders for safety; internally displaced persons (IDPs) flee within their own country due to similar threats.

Protection

Non-refoulement

A core principle: people should not be returned to places where they face serious harm or persecution.

Community

Resettlement and integration

Stable housing, language access, and employment pathways enable refugees to thrive and contribute to local economies and culture.

Take action

Donate

Contribute to vetted organizations providing housing, legal support, education, and emergency relief. Consider recurring gifts to sustain long-term programs.

Volunteer

Offer language tutoring, mentorship, or job coaching. Partner with local groups to support newcomer families and help navigate services.

Advocate

Engage with policymakers on fair asylum processes, family reunification, and community-based integration.

Amplify

Share verified stories and data. Center refugee voices and creativity through events, art, and media.
